# Grand Chocolate Souffle

## Introduction

Souffle exposes every small kitchen habit: whether you whip patiently, fold lightly, serve on time. Name comes from French verb meaning "to puff"; by nineteenth century it was classic French showpiece. History can seem forbidding, but good souffle is well-built base lifted by carefully whipped egg whites, protected by steady heat.

This grand chocolate version is both impressive and deeply flavorful. Instead of chasing height alone, it builds dark, glossy base so center tastes like warm chocolate mousse while crown rises light and dry. Sort of dessert that makes table go quiet for moment.

## Ingredients List

- 170 g bittersweet chocolate, ~70% cacao. Valrhona or Guittard melt smoothly and taste clean; substitute semisweet for softer chocolate edge, but avoid chips.
- 30 g unsalted butter, plus more for ramekins. Kerrygold or Plugra add good flavor; salted butter works but use lighter hand with added salt.
- 50 g granulated sugar, plus 25 g for coating dishes. Fine cane sugar dissolves easily; regular white sugar is fine, but coarse sugar can leave foam heavy.
- 4 large eggs, separated. Fresh eggs whip more reliably; if eggs are very small, use 5.
- 120 ml whole milk. Gives base body; 2% is acceptable, but skim makes mixture too thin.
- 12 g all-purpose flour. King Arthur is dependable; neutral gluten-free blend can substitute.
- 1 teaspoon pure vanilla extract. Nielsen-Massey is excellent; vanilla bean paste also works.
- 1 pinch fine sea salt. Blends in gently; table salt is sharper, so use slightly less.
- 1/8 teaspoon cream of tartar. Stabilizes whites; few drops of lemon juice can stand in.
- Confectioners' sugar or cocoa powder for finishing, optional.

## Equipment

- Four straight-sided ramekins, 180–240 ml each, so batter climbs upward.
- Pastry brush or paper towel for even buttering.
- Saucepan and heatproof bowl, or double boiler, for gentle melting.
- Whisk and silicone spatula for building and folding base.
- Stand mixer or hand mixer for stable, even egg whites.
- Sheet pan for quick, steady transfer into oven.

## Technique

### Prepare ramekins and oven

Heat oven to 200 C with rack in lower-middle position. Butter each ramekin thoroughly, brushing upward from bottom to rim. Upward stroke helps batter grip sides as it rises. Add spoonful of sugar to each dish, rotate to coat interior completely, tap out excess. Bare spots can cause sticking; thick patches can create uneven rise.

### Build chocolate base

Warm milk until steaming. In saucepan, cook flour and butter together over low heat until smooth and lightly blond, then whisk in hot milk. This quick roux gives souffle enough structure to hold air from egg whites. Keep whisking until mixture thickens to consistency of pastry cream. Off heat, add chopped chocolate, vanilla, and salt; stir until glossy and fluid. Cool few minutes, then whisk in egg yolks one at time. Base should feel warm, not hot; too hot can cook yolks and later flatten whites.

### Whip egg whites

Place whites in perfectly clean bowl and beat until foamy. Add cream of tartar, then slowly rain in sugar as whites become opaque. Gradual sugar addition strengthens foam without making it grainy. Beat to medium-stiff peaks: tip should bend slightly when you lift whisk. Whites look dull or clumpy → overwhipped and harder to fold. Whites slump like loose soap bubbles → need more time. Proper whites look glossy and silky.

### Fold and fill

Stir one-third of whites into chocolate base to lighten it. Fold in rest in two additions with spatula: cut down through center, sweep along bottom, lift batter over itself while rotating bowl. Aim is to preserve as much air as possible. Stop when only few faint pale streaks remain. Batter should look like airy mousse, not dense pudding. Spoon into prepared ramekins, level tops, run thumb around inside rim to make shallow channel. That trench helps souffle rise evenly and form clean crown.

### Bake and judge doneness

Set ramekins on sheet pan and bake 14–17 minutes. Don't open oven door during first 12 minutes; blast of cool air can stop lift before structure sets. Finished souffle should stand above rim, with lightly dry top and edges that feel set while center still quivers slightly. Aroma should be deeply chocolaty, almost like warm cake batter and cocoa. Rises poorly → foam was likely deflated or underwhipped. Browns too fast → oven runs hot.

## Timing Breakdown

- Prep: 20 min
- Active: 25 min
- Cool/rest: 10 min
- Bake: 14–17 min
- Total: ~45–50 min

## Serving Instructions

Serve immediately. Clock starts moment souffles leave oven. Dust tops with confectioners' sugar or cocoa, bring straight to table. Wonderful with softly whipped cream, creme anglaise, or few raspberries for tart contrast. Let each guest break top with spoon and release fragrant steam from center.

## Troubleshooting

- Falls fast → probably underbaked or oven door opened too early.
- Center raw → ramekins may be larger than expected or oven runs cool.
- Dense texture → whites underwhipped or overfolded.
- Crooked rise → ramekins not evenly buttered and sugared.
- Grainy base → overheated during melting.

## Chef's Notes

For deeper flavor, add 1 tsp espresso powder to milk or 1 tbsp dark rum to cooled chocolate base. Winter: tiny pinch of cinnamon. Summer: serve with cherries or berries. Can prepare ramekins and base ahead, but whip and fold whites only just before baking. That's real secret.
