# Grand Chocolate Soufflé

## Introduction

Soufflé exposes every kitchen habit: whipping patience, folding lightness, serving timing. Name from French "to puff" — nineteenth-century classic showpiece. Sounds forbidding, but it's a well-built base lifted by whipped egg whites and steady heat.

This chocolate version builds dark, glossy base so center tastes like warm chocolate mousse while crown rises light and dry. Dessert that makes a table go quiet.

## Ingredients

- 170 g bittersweet chocolate, ~70% cacao. Valrhona or Guittard melt smoothly; semisweet for softer edge, avoid chips.
- 30 g unsalted butter, plus more for ramekins. Kerrygold or Plugra for flavor; salted works with less added salt.
- 50 g granulated sugar, plus 25 g for coating. Fine cane dissolves easily; coarse leaves foam heavy.
- 4 large eggs, separated. Fresh whip more reliably; very small eggs → use 5.
- 120 ml whole milk. Gives base body; 2% acceptable, skim too thin.
- 12 g all-purpose flour. King Arthur dependable; gluten-free blend substitutes.
- 1 tsp pure vanilla extract. Nielsen-Massey excellent; bean paste works too.
- 1 pinch fine sea salt. Blends gently; table salt sharper, use less.
- 1/8 tsp cream of tartar. Stabilizes whites; lemon juice can stand in.
- Confectioners' sugar or cocoa for finishing, optional.

## Equipment

- Four straight-sided ramekins, 180–240 ml. Batter climbs upward.
- Pastry brush or paper towel for even buttering.
- Saucepan + heatproof bowl (or double boiler) for gentle melting.
- Whisk + silicone spatula for building and folding base.
- Stand mixer or hand mixer for stable egg whites.
- Sheet pan for steady oven transfer.

## Technique

### Ramekins and oven

Heat oven to 200°C, rack in lower-middle. Butter each ramekin thoroughly, brushing upward — helps batter grip sides as it rises. Spoonful of sugar, rotate to coat, tap excess. Bare spots cause sticking; thick patches create uneven rise.

### Chocolate base

Warm milk until steaming. Cook flour and butter over low heat until smooth and lightly blond, whisk in hot milk. This roux gives structure to hold air from egg whites. Whisk until consistency of pastry cream. Off heat, add chopped chocolate, vanilla, salt — stir until glossy and fluid. Cool few minutes, whisk in yolks one at a time. Base should feel warm, not hot — too hot cooks yolks and later flattens whites.

### Egg whites

Whites in clean bowl, beat until foamy. Add cream of tartar, slowly rain in sugar as whites become opaque. Gradual addition strengthens foam without making it grainy. Beat to medium-stiff peaks: tip bends slightly on lifted whisk. Dull/clumpy = overwhipped, harder to fold. Slumping like soap bubbles = needs more time. Proper whites: glossy, silky.

### Fold and fill

Stir one-third whites into chocolate base to lighten. Fold rest in two additions — cut down through center, sweep along bottom, lift batter over itself, rotate bowl. Aim: preserve maximum air. Stop at few faint pale streaks. Batter should look like airy mousse, not dense pudding. Spoon into ramekins, level tops, thumb around inside rim for shallow channel — helps even rise and clean crown.

### Bake

Ramekins on sheet pan, 14–17 minutes. Don't open oven first 12 minutes — cool air stops lift before structure sets. Finished: stands above rim, lightly dry top, set edges, center still quivers. Aroma: deeply chocolaty, like warm cake batter and cocoa. Poor rise = deflated or underwhipped foam. Browns too fast = oven runs hot.

## Timing

- Prep: 20 min
- Active: 25 min
- Cooling/resting: 10 min
- Bake: 14–17 min
- Total: ~45–50 min

## Serving

Serve immediately — clock starts when soufflés leave oven. Dust with confectioners' sugar or cocoa, bring straight to table. Pairs with softly whipped cream, crème anglaise, or raspberries for tart contrast. Each guest breaks top with spoon, releasing fragrant steam from center.

## Troubleshooting

- Falls fast → underbaked or oven door opened too early
- Raw center → ramekins larger than expected or oven runs cool
- Dense texture → whites underwhipped or overfolded
- Crooked rise → uneven buttering/sugaring
- Grainy base → overheated during melting

## Chef's Notes

Deeper flavor: 1 tsp espresso powder in milk or 1 tbsp dark rum in cooled base. Winter: tiny pinch cinnamon. Summer: serve with cherries or berries. Ramekins and base can prep ahead — whip and fold whites only before baking. Real secret of soufflé: timing.
