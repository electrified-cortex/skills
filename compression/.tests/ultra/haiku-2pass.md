# Grand Chocolate Souffle

Intro:
Souffle exposes kitchen habits: whipping patience, folding lightness, timing. Name from French "to puff," 19th-century classic. Reputation forbidding but it's well-built base lifted by whipped egg whites + steady heat.

This version builds dark, glossy base → center tastes like warm chocolate mousse, crown rises light and dry. Dessert that quiets a table.

Ingredients:
170 g bittersweet chocolate, ~70% cacao. Valrhona/Guittard melt smooth; semisweet OK for softer edge, avoid chips.
30 g unsalted butter + more for ramekins. Kerrygold/Plugra good; salted works but reduce added salt.
50 g granulated sugar + 25 g for coating dishes. Fine cane dissolves easily; coarse sugar → heavy foam.
4 large eggs, separated. Fresh whip better; if very small, use 5.
120 ml whole milk. Gives base body; 2% acceptable, skim too thin.
12 g AP flour. King Arthur dependable; neutral GF blend substitutes.
1 tsp pure vanilla extract. Nielsen-Massey excellent; vanilla bean paste works.
1 pinch fine sea salt. Table salt sharper → use less.
1/8 tsp cream of tartar. Stabilizes whites; lemon juice substitutes.
Confectioners' sugar or cocoa for finishing, optional.

Equipment:
Four straight-sided ramekins, 180–240 ml each; pastry brush or paper towel for buttering; saucepan + heatproof bowl (or double boiler); whisk + silicone spatula; stand mixer or hand mixer; sheet pan.

Technique:

Prepare ramekins and oven:
Heat oven 200 C, rack lower-middle. Butter each ramekin brushing upward bottom to rim — upward stroke helps batter grip sides. Spoon sugar in, rotate to coat, tap excess. Bare spots → sticking; thick patches → uneven rise.

Build chocolate base:
Warm milk until steaming. Cook flour + butter over low heat until smooth and lightly blond, whisk in hot milk. Quick roux gives structure to hold air from whites. Whisk until pastry-cream consistency. Off heat, add chopped chocolate, vanilla, salt — stir until glossy and fluid. Cool few min, whisk in egg yolks one at time. Base should feel warm, not hot; too hot → cooks yolks and flattens whites.

Whip egg whites:
Whites in clean bowl, beat until foamy. Add cream of tartar, slowly rain in sugar as whites become opaque. Gradual sugar strengthens foam without graininess. Beat to medium-stiff peaks: tip bends slightly when whisk lifts. Dull/clumpy → overwhipped, harder to fold; slumping like loose soap bubbles → needs more time. Proper whites look glossy and silky.

Fold and fill:
Stir 1/3 whites into chocolate base to lighten. Fold rest in two additions — spatula cuts down center, sweeps along bottom, lifts batter over itself while rotating bowl. Preserve max air. Stop when only faint pale streaks remain. Batter should look like airy mousse, not dense pudding. Spoon into ramekins, level tops, run thumb around inside rim → shallow channel helps even rise and clean crown.

Bake and judge doneness:
Ramekins on sheet pan, bake 14–17 min. Don't open oven first 12 min; cool air stops lift before structure sets. Done souffle stands above rim, lightly dry top, set edges, center still quivers slightly. Aroma deeply chocolaty — warm cake batter and cocoa. Rises poorly → foam deflated or underwhipped. Browns too fast → oven runs hot.

Timing:
Prep: 20 min; Active: 25 min; Cooling/resting: 10 min; Bake: 14–17 min; Total: ~45–50 min.

Serving:
Serve immediately — clock starts when souffles leave oven. Dust tops with confectioners' sugar or cocoa, bring straight to table. Pair with whipped cream, creme anglaise, or raspberries for tart contrast. Each guest breaks top with spoon, releasing fragrant steam from center.

Troubleshooting:
Falls fast → underbaked or oven door opened too early.
Center raw → ramekins larger than expected or oven runs cool.
Dense texture → whites underwhipped or overfolded.
Crooked rise → ramekins unevenly buttered/sugared.
Grainy base → overheated during melting.

Chef's Notes:
Deeper flavor: add 1 tsp espresso powder to milk or 1 tbsp dark rum to cooled base. Winter: tiny pinch cinnamon. Summer: serve with cherries/berries. Ramekins and base can prep ahead, but whip and fold whites only just before baking — that's real secret.
