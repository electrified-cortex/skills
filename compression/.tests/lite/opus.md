# Grand Chocolate Soufflé

## Introduction

The soufflé exposes every kitchen habit: whether you whip patiently, fold lightly, and serve on time. Its name comes from the French verb meaning "to puff," and by the nineteenth century it had become a classic French showpiece. A soufflé is a well-built base lifted by whipped egg whites and protected by steady heat.

This grand chocolate version builds a dark, glossy base so the center tastes like warm chocolate mousse while the crown rises light and dry. It's the dessert that makes a table go quiet.

## Ingredients List

- 170 g bittersweet chocolate, about 70 percent cacao. Valrhona or Guittard melt smoothly and taste clean; substitute semisweet for a softer edge, but avoid chips.
- 30 g unsalted butter, plus more for the ramekins. Kerrygold or Plugra add good flavor; salted butter works, but use a lighter hand with added salt.
- 50 g granulated sugar, plus 25 g for coating the dishes. Fine cane sugar dissolves easily; coarse sugar can leave the foam heavy.
- 4 large eggs, separated. Fresh eggs whip more reliably; if your eggs are very small, use 5.
- 120 ml whole milk. Whole milk gives the base body; 2 percent is acceptable, but skim makes the mixture too thin.
- 12 g all-purpose flour. King Arthur is dependable; a neutral gluten-free blend can substitute.
- 1 teaspoon pure vanilla extract. Nielsen-Massey is excellent; vanilla bean paste also works.
- 1 pinch fine sea salt. Fine sea salt blends gently; table salt is sharper, so use less.
- 1/8 teaspoon cream of tartar. This stabilizes the whites; a few drops of lemon juice can stand in.
- Confectioners' sugar or cocoa powder for finishing, optional.

## Equipment

- Four straight-sided ramekins, 180 to 240 ml each, so the batter climbs upward.
- A pastry brush or paper towel for even buttering.
- A saucepan and heatproof bowl, or double boiler, for gentle melting.
- A whisk and silicone spatula for building and folding the base.
- A stand mixer or hand mixer for stable, even egg whites.
- A sheet pan for steady transfer into the oven.

## Technique

### Prepare the ramekins and oven

Heat the oven to 200 C with a rack in the lower-middle position. Butter each ramekin thoroughly, brushing upward from bottom to rim. The upward stroke helps the batter grip the sides as it rises. Add a spoonful of sugar to each dish, rotate to coat the interior, and tap out the excess. Bare spots can cause sticking; thick patches can create an uneven rise.

### Build the chocolate base

Warm the milk until steaming. Cook the flour and butter together over low heat until smooth and lightly blond, then whisk in the hot milk. This quick roux gives the souffle enough structure to hold air from the egg whites. Keep whisking until it thickens to pastry cream consistency. Off the heat, add the chopped chocolate, vanilla, and salt; stir until glossy and fluid. Let it cool a few minutes, then whisk in the egg yolks one at a time. The base should feel warm, not hot — if it's too hot, it can cook the yolks and later flatten the whites.

### Whip the egg whites

Place the whites in a clean bowl and beat until foamy. Add the cream of tartar, then slowly rain in the sugar as the whites become opaque. Gradual sugar addition strengthens the foam without making it grainy. Beat to medium-stiff peaks: the tip should bend slightly when you lift the whisk. If the whites look dull or clumpy, they're overwhipped and harder to fold; if they slump like loose soap bubbles, they need more time. Proper whites look glossy and silky.

### Fold and fill

Stir one-third of the whites into the chocolate base to lighten it. Fold in the rest in two additions with a spatula, cutting down through the center, sweeping along the bottom, and lifting the batter over itself while rotating the bowl. The aim is to preserve as much air as possible. Stop when only a few faint pale streaks remain. The batter should look like airy mousse, not dense pudding. Spoon into the prepared ramekins, level the tops, and run your thumb around the inside rim to make a shallow channel. That trench helps the soufflé rise evenly and form a clean crown.

### Bake and judge doneness

Set the ramekins on a sheet pan and bake for 14 to 17 minutes. Don't open the oven door during the first 12 minutes; a blast of cool air can stop the lift before the structure sets. A finished souffle should stand above the rim, with a lightly dry top and edges that feel set while the center still quivers. The aroma should be deeply chocolaty, like warm cake batter and cocoa. If it rises poorly, the foam was likely deflated or underwhipped. If it browns too fast, your oven runs hot.

## Timing Breakdown

- Prep time: 20 minutes
- Active time: 25 minutes
- Cooling and resting: 10 minutes
- Bake time: 14 to 17 minutes
- Total: about 45 to 50 minutes

## Serving Instructions

Serve immediately. The clock starts when the souffles leave the oven. Dust the tops with confectioners' sugar or cocoa and bring them straight to the table. They pair well with softly whipped cream, crème anglaise, or a few raspberries for tart contrast. Each guest breaks the top with a spoon to release the fragrant steam from the center.

## Troubleshooting

- If the soufflé falls fast, it was likely underbaked or the oven door was opened too early.
- If the center is raw, the ramekins may be larger than expected or the oven may run cool.
- If the texture is dense, the whites were underwhipped or overfolded.
- If the rise is crooked, the ramekins weren't evenly buttered and sugared.
- If the chocolate base turns grainy, it was overheated during melting.

## Chef's Notes

For deeper flavor, add 1 teaspoon espresso powder to the milk or 1 tablespoon dark rum to the cooled chocolate base. In winter, a tiny pinch of cinnamon is excellent; in summer, serve with cherries or berries. You can prepare the ramekins and base ahead, but whip and fold the whites only before baking. That's the real secret.
