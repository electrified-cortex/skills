Grand Chocolate Souffle

Souffle exposes kitchen habits: whipping patience, folding lightness, serving timing. Name from French "to puff" — 19th-century classic. Good souffle = well-built base + carefully whipped whites + steady heat.

This version prioritizes flavor over height alone. Dark glossy base → center tastes like warm chocolate mousse; crown rises light and dry. Dessert that quiets a table.

Ingredients:
170 g bittersweet chocolate, ~70% cacao (Valrhona/Guittard; semisweet OK, avoid chips)
30 g unsalted butter + more for ramekins (Kerrygold/Plugra; salted works — less added salt)
50 g granulated sugar + 25 g for coating (fine cane dissolves easily; coarse makes foam heavy)
4 large eggs, separated (fresh whip better; use 5 if very small)
120 ml whole milk (2% acceptable; skim too thin)
12 g all-purpose flour (King Arthur; GF blend substitutes)
1 tsp pure vanilla extract (Nielsen-Massey; vanilla bean paste works)
1 pinch fine sea salt (table salt sharper — use less)
1/8 tsp cream of tartar (stabilizes whites; lemon juice substitutes)
Confectioners' sugar or cocoa for finishing, optional

Equipment:
4 straight-sided ramekins, 180–240 ml each
Pastry brush or paper towel for buttering
Saucepan + heatproof bowl or double boiler
Whisk + silicone spatula
Stand mixer or hand mixer
Sheet pan

Technique:

Prepare ramekins and oven:
Heat oven 200°C, rack lower-middle. Butter each ramekin brushing upward bottom→rim — upward stroke helps batter grip sides. Spoon sugar in, rotate to coat interior, tap excess. Bare spots → sticking; thick patches → uneven rise.

Build chocolate base:
Warm milk til steaming. Cook flour+butter over low heat til smooth and lightly blond, whisk in hot milk. Quick roux gives structure to hold egg white air. Whisk til pastry-cream consistency. Off heat, add chopped chocolate, vanilla, salt — stir til glossy and fluid. Cool few minutes, whisk in yolks one at a time. Base should feel warm not hot — too hot cooks yolks and flattens whites.

Whip egg whites:
Whites in perfectly clean bowl, beat til foamy. Add cream of tartar, slowly rain in sugar as whites become opaque. Gradual sugar strengthens foam without graininess. Beat to medium-stiff peaks: tip bends slightly when whisk lifts. Dull/clumpy = overwhipped, harder to fold. Slumping like loose soap bubbles = need more time. Proper whites look glossy and silky.

Fold and fill:
Stir 1/3 whites into chocolate base to lighten. Fold rest in 2 additions — spatula cuts down through center, sweeps along bottom, lifts batter over itself while rotating bowl. Goal: preserve max air. Stop when only faint pale streaks remain. Batter should look like airy mousse, not dense pudding. Spoon into ramekins, level tops, thumb around inside rim → shallow channel helps even rise and clean crown.

Bake and judge doneness:
Ramekins on sheet pan, bake 14–17 min. Don't open door first 12 min — cool air stops lift before structure sets. Done souffle stands above rim: lightly dry top, set edges, center still quivers slightly. Aroma deeply chocolaty — warm cake batter + cocoa. Poor rise → foam deflated or underwhipped. Browns too fast → oven runs hot.

Timing:
Prep: 20 min
Active: 25 min
Cooling/rest: 10 min
Bake: 14–17 min
Total: ~45–50 min

Serving:
Serve immediately — clock starts when souffles leave oven. Dust tops w/ confectioners' sugar or cocoa, bring straight to table. Pair w/ softly whipped cream, crème anglaise, or raspberries for tart contrast. Each guest breaks top w/ spoon, releases fragrant steam from center.

Troubleshooting:
Falls fast → underbaked or door opened too early
Center raw → ramekins larger than expected or oven runs cool
Dense texture → whites underwhipped or overfolded
Crooked rise → ramekins unevenly buttered/sugared
Grainy base → overheated during melting

Notes:
Deeper flavor: add 1 tsp espresso powder to milk or 1 tbsp dark rum to cooled base. Winter: tiny pinch cinnamon. Summer: serve w/ cherries or berries. Ramekins and base can prep ahead — whip and fold whites only just before baking. That's real secret.
